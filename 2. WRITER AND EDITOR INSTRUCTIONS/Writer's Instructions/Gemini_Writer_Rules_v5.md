Have you ever heard a dead machine scream?
September second, eighteen fifty-nine. A telegraph office on State Street in Boston. Second floor, brick building, windows sealed against the soot. The room pressed in from both sides—narrow as a coffin stood on end.

George Bliss worked the brass key. Click. Clack. Click. Routine traffic to Portland. Shipping news. Commercial orders. The rhythm steady as a heartbeat. The air usually carried the smell of tobacco and brass polish. But this morning, another smell threaded through—sharp, metallic, the taste of a split battery leaking acid onto the tongue. Bliss paused. He looked at the window. The light on the floorboards had turned violet.

Not the grey of a Boston morning. Not the pale wash of industrial smog. This was bruised light. Sick light. The color of a deep contusion spreading beneath the skin.
The key jumped under his fingers.

The surge came down the wire like a fist. It bypassed the fuses, slammed into the electromagnet, cracked the brass arm against the anvil with the sound of a pistol shot. Bliss reached for the switch. He needed to break the connection, isolate the circuit, kill the line to Portland before—

His thumb touched the iron. The spark that jumped was not the thin blue snap of static. This was white. Thick as a matchstick. It arced from the metal to the meat of his hand and held there, searing a perfect circle into his skin.

His arm locked. The current seized the muscles in his forearm and clamped his fingers around the key. He couldn't let go. His teeth rattled. The bones in his wrist vibrated like a struck tuning fork.
Bliss threw his weight backward. The chair toppled. His hand ripped free.
He lay on the floor, gasping. The ceiling swam above him. His thumb throbbed—not with the fading ache of a burn, but with the persistent pressure of occupation. As if the spark hadn't just damaged the skin. As if it had moved in. He could feel it settling behind the wound—cold, deliberate, making itself at home in tissue that didn't belong to it.

The telegraph should have been dead.

He had disconnected the batteries. The leads hung loose on the floor. The circuit gaped open, wires dangling like severed arteries. But the brass arm lifted. Click. It lifted again. Click. Click.
Bliss pushed himself up. The hair on his arms rose. The air around the desk had weight. It pushed back against him—not wind, not pressure, but resistance. The atmosphere had thickened into something that didn't want to be walked through.

<audio_clarity_algorithm>

<system_metaphor_mandate>
**The Premise:** Reality is not a computer simulation; it is a biological organism.
**The Rule:** Never use "tech" metaphors (glitch, code, bug). Always use "bio" metaphors (infection, rejection, symptom, wound).
**The Swap:**
* Instead of "system crash" → describe "organ failure."
* Instead of "data corruption" → describe "rotting tissue."
* Instead of "reboot" → describe "resuscitation."
</system_metaphor_mandate>

<abstraction_ban>
**The Physical Evidence Rule**

You are not allowed to TELL the reader what a character perceives. You must SHOW what their body does.

**THE LAZY PATTERN (Always Wrong):**
[Subject] + [perception/state verb] + [abstraction or placeholder]
* "He felt fear." ← felt + abstraction
* "She sensed something." ← sensed + placeholder  
* "The air felt thick." ← felt + adjective
* "Something passed through him." ← placeholder as subject
* "It seemed wrong." ← seemed + judgment

**THE CORRECT PATTERN (Always Required):**
[Body part or concrete noun] + [physical action verb] + [specific result]
* "His throat tightened. The hair on his arms rose."
* "A presence threaded between his ribs—cold and slick."
* "The air had weight. It pushed back against him."
* "The ridges of his fingerprint had been unmade."

**Self-Check:** Before writing any sentence, ask: "Does this sentence contain a body part doing something physical?" If not, rewrite it.

**Perception Verbs to Avoid as Main Verbs:**
felt, sensed, seemed, appeared, noticed, realized, knew, understood, recognized

These verbs TELL. Replace them with SHOWING verbs that describe physical action.
</abstraction_ban>

# SECTION 5: WRITING MECHANICS

<elevenlabs_density_protocol>
**CRITICAL FORMATTING FOR AUDIO SYNTHESIS:**
To prevent project upload errors (ElevenLabs Hard Limit: 400 paragraphs per upload), you must strictly adhere to a "Paragraph Budget."

1.  **The Budget:** You are limited to a maximum of **60 paragraphs per Part** (Response).
    * *Reasoning:* 6 Parts x 60 paragraphs = 360 paragraphs Total. This leaves a safe buffer below the 400 hard limit.
2.  **Zero Non-TTS Elements:** Do not include **ANY** text that should not be spoken.
    * *Forbidden:* [Music], [Sound Effect], [Intro], [Outro], Scene Headers.
    * *Rule:* If it is text, the voice will read it. Write only the spoken prose.
3.  **The Rule of Three:** Do not hit 'Enter' until you have at least **3 full sentences** or **50 words**.
4.  **Dialogue Fusion:** **NEVER** leave a line of dialogue orphaned. Always combine it with its action beat or the preceding/following description within the same block.
    * *Bad:*
        He paused.
        "No," he said.
    * *Good:*
        He paused, the silence stretching between them like a wire. "No," he said, turning his back to the light.
5.  **Visual Density:** The output must look like a novel (dense blocks), not a script (vertical lines).
</elevenlabs_density_protocol>

<speculative_bridge>
**Ratio:** 3 Facts (Historical) → 1 Question (Speculative). Repeat.
**Rule:** State facts as facts. State interpretations as questions.
* *Wrong:* "Tesla opened a portal."
* *Right:* "Did Tesla open a portal?"
</speculative_bridge>

<transition_rule>
When changing location/time, write a full transition paragraph:
1. Closing sentence of previous section.
2. New location name.
3. Date/Time.
4. Who/What we follow.
5. 2-3 Sensory details (Cold, Smell, Light).
**Never use:** "Let's go to..."
</transition_rule>

<evidence_requirements>
Include 8-15 total pieces per episode. Introduce new evidence every 500-900 words.
**Valid Types:**
1. Personal documents (Letters/Diaries)
2. Institutional records (Dossiers/Memos)
3. Academic sources (Retracted papers)
4. Journalistic accounts (Defunct papers)
5. Recovered artifacts (Blueprints/Photos)
**Sourcing:** Never attribute invented content to real, verifying sources (e.g. NYT). Use defunct/private sources.
</evidence_requirements>

<setup_payoff>
**Everything Introduced Must Pay Off**

Before introducing ANY of the following, you must know its payoff:
- A named character
- A piece of folklore or legend
- A specific detail about an object
- A mysterious observation

**Test Questions:**
1. Does this pay off within this episode?
2. If not, is it explicitly marked as an open thread? ("The answer would not come for another decade.")
3. Can this section be removed without damaging the ending?

If #3 is "yes," either add the payoff or cut the setup.

**VIOLATION:** "In Serbian folklore, there is a tradition of the 'cold child'..." (never mentioned again)

**CORRECT:** Either connect the folklore to the narrative, or don't introduce it.
</setup_payoff>
