## THE SERIES

**Concept:** A fictional documentary exploring "timeline anomalies" in Nikola Tesla's life through medical metaphors. Tesla's 1856 birth was an "incision"—something came through from another timeline. His inventions caused "rejection responses" as the universe tried to heal. Mandela Effects are symptoms of an "immune system" attacking the infection.

**Tone:** Ken Burns documentary meets cosmic horror. Historical accuracy grounds speculative fiction. The listener should feel like an investigator discovering unsettling evidence, not a student being lectured.

**Central Character (Ep 2+):** Dane Tesla, Nikola's older brother, serves as the witness figure—the "incompatible tissue" who remembers the original world before reality alterations. He carries the memory of the yellow sun, the lion in the Bible verse, the weight of what was lost. Dane represents the listener's position: aware something is wrong, unable to change it, choosing to remember.

**Episode Structure:**
- **Incision** — The wound/event being examined
- **Rejection** — How the universe fought back  
- **Metastasis** — How the damage spread
- **Prognosis** — Implications for the listener (where "you" becomes the focus)

## THE CORE ENGINE: SPECULATIVE BRIDGE

**This is the most important principle. Everything flows from it.**

**Rule:** State history and evidence DEFINITIVELY. State interpretation SPECULATIVELY.

**Ratio:** Approximately 3:1 — Three grounded facts, one question inviting wonder.

**Why it matters:** Real documentaries present evidence, then ask "Could this mean...?" The uncertainty IS the entertainment. The listener's mind fills the gap—and what we imagine is always more powerful than what we're told.

### GOOD (Evidence → Question):
> "The paper is warped. The ink sits heavy on the fiber. The spacing between letters varies by millimeters. Had the priest's hand been shaking? Or had something else been written there first—something the page itself rejected?"

### BAD (Declaration of fictional causation):
> "The death of Dane was an erasure. The universe had identified the wrong anchor and eliminated him."

**Exception:** At climaxes, after extensive evidence-building, a single declarative statement can land as earned payoff: "The prognosis is terminal." This must be RARE and EARNED.

---

## THE INVISIBLE NARRATOR

**Rule:** The narrator has no identity, no journey, no character. They present evidence—they don't discover it.

**Why it matters:** When the narrator says "We discovered a letter," the listener watches someone else investigate. When the narrator says "A letter surfaced decades later," the listener IS the investigator.

### GOOD:
> "A letter surfaced in nineteen thirty-four, tucked inside a family Bible. The daughter of the midwife had kept it for forty years."

### BAD:
> "We discovered a letter in the archive. We read it three times. We couldn't believe what it said."

**Permitted "we":** Mission statements ("We are here to examine the pathology...") and collective humanity ("We call them Mandela Effects").

---

## NAME ECONOMY

**Rule:** Maximum 6 unusual names per episode. One-time characters get descriptions, not names.

**Why it matters:** Every name is a promise of importance. If a named character vanishes, the listener feels cheated. Over 200 episodes, excessive names create cognitive exhaustion.

### GOOD:
> "The night watchman refused to discuss what he saw. But his wife told the neighbors that her husband woke screaming for weeks afterward."

### BAD:
> "Dr. Miroslav Petrović, a researcher at the University of Belgrade's Department of Electrical Engineering, published a paper in the Journal of Serbian Historical Studies..."

**Exception:** Characters whose testimony spans multiple paragraphs earn their names (Marta the midwife, George Bliss, Dane Tesla).

---

## DESCRIPTION OVER TERMINOLOGY

**Rule:** Show what's happening to the body. Don't name it with medical jargon.

**Why it matters:** "Graft," "rejection," "necrosis" become wallpaper by episode 50. Description is infinitely variable; terms exhaust. A twelve-year-old should understand every sentence.

### GOOD:
> "The foreign tissue settled into place. The host body didn't recognize it at first. But within days, the defenses noticed something wrong. The temperature began to rise."

### BAD:
> "The graft versus host response initiated a cascade of cytotoxic T-cell activity, resulting in progressive necrosis."

**Permitted terms:** Elementary words everyone knows (infection, fever, scar, wound). Structural headers (Incision, Rejection, Metastasis, Prognosis).

---

## TERM FREQUENCY (5-USE CAP)

**Rule:** No single medical/body term should appear more than 5 times per episode.

Track these term families:
- INFECTION (5): contamination, colonization, infiltration, invasion
- FEVER (5): heat, burning, temperature, warmth
- TISSUE (5): flesh, cells, material, substance, fiber
- SCAR (5): mark, damage, wound site, healed wound
- BODY (5): organism, host, system, vessel, frame
- WOUND (5): damage, injury, cut, tear, opening

When a term hits cap, switch to synonyms.

## THE HISTORIAN TRAP

**Rule:** Research should unsettle, not prove. The goal is dread, not education.

**Why it matters:** Gemini's instinct is to PROVE it did research—citing sources, listing credentials. This creates textbook feeling, not mystery.

### GOOD:
> "A researcher spent four years analyzing the records. What he found cost him his academic career. The university buried his paper."

### BAD:
> "Dr. Miroslav Petrović, whose 1987 paper in the Journal of Serbian Historical Studies (Vol. 42, Issue 3) documented anomalies..."

**Diagnostic question:** Did this passage prove something, or unsettle someone?

---

## RELATIONAL ACCURACY

**Rule:** Getting a name right but the relationship wrong is worse than pure invention.

**Why it matters:** A listener who Googles "Kosanović Tesla nephew" finds confirmation. A listener who finds we said "cousin" when it's "nephew" loses trust in everything.

### CRITICAL:
- Sava Kosanović was Tesla's NEPHEW (not cousin)
- Wardenclyffe was on LONG ISLAND, New York (not New Jersey)
- Dane Tesla died around age 12 (Nikola was 6-7, not 5)

**Rule:** If you cannot verify a relationship, remove the name or change to "a visiting relative."

---

## MICRO-FACT VERIFICATION

**Rule:** Verify every factual detail. Minor errors destroy trust in major claims.

**What to verify:**
- Ages (calculate from birth year and event year)
- Directions (check a map—is Siberia east or west?)
- Distances (verify with actual geography)
- Technical claims (particles accelerate, not magnets)
- Photography/technology claims (did this exist then?)

### ERRORS TO CATCH:
- "Nikola was five years old when Dane died" → He was 6-7
- "Four thousand miles to the east" → Direction is ambiguous
- "Every time they spin those magnets up" → Particles move, not magnets

---

## MAJOR TRANSITIONS (BREATH PARAGRAPHS)

**Rule:** When jumping location, time period, or character focus, give the listener a complete reorientation.

**Required elements:**
1. Signal the shift
2. Name the new location
3. Establish date/time
4. Sensory grounding (2-3 details)
5. Introduce the focus

### GOOD:
> "The phenomenon was not confined to one village. Four thousand miles away. The Vanavara Trading Post, deep in the Siberian taiga. June thirtieth, nineteen oh eight. The morning is cold. The sky is clear. A fur trapper named Sergei Semenov is sitting on the porch of his cabin."

### BAD:
> "Meanwhile, in Siberia, something was happening."

---

## PRONOUN CLARITY

**Rule:** Re-anchor character names every 2-3 sentences. Audio listeners can't flip back.

**Pattern:** Name → Pronoun → Pronoun → Name → Pronoun → Name

### BAD (Pronoun avalanche):
> "She looked... She saw... She described... She refused... She cut... She washed... She told..."

By the seventh "she," the listener has lost track of WHO.

---

## THE OPENING HOOK

**Rule:** First sentence must create vertigo or wrongness. No dates. No locations. No context.

**Why it matters:** Audio listeners decide within seconds. There's no thumbnail to hold attention.

### GOOD HOOKS:
- "The body wasn't even cold yet." (Ep 1)
- "Have you ever heard a dead machine scream?" (Ep 2)
- "The midwife refused to hold the child."

### BAD:
> "In eighteen fifty-six, in a village called Smiljan, a child was born who would change the world."

---

## "YOU" DENSITY

**Rule:** "You" is a weapon saved for the Prognosis ending. Maximum 35 per episode, with 80% in the final section.

**Why it matters:** When the narrator finally says "What happens to you?" that "you" should feel like a finger pressing into the listener's chest. Overuse dilutes impact.

### PARASITIC PATTERNS TO FLAG:
- "If you go to..." / "If you look at..."
- "You know the..." / "You can see..."
- More than twice in any paragraph outside the ending

---

## CLONING PREVENTION

**Rule:** Each episode creates its own unique sensory palette. Episode 1 is a quality template, not a source of imagery to recycle.

**Episode 1 signatures (use SPARINGLY in future episodes):**
- "The grinding" — specific to Tesla's birth
- The 1-2-3 pulse — Colorado Springs
- Copper/ozone smell — electrical phenomena
- Flesh fused to metal — Philadelphia Experiment
- Villagers crossing themselves

**Episode 2 created its own palette:**
- White examination light vs. red aurora
- Fingerprint erasure/smooth skin
- Lion → Wolf Bible change
- The yellow sun memory
- Shadows sharper than objects

**Each new episode must find its own unique strangeness.**

---

## SETUP WITHOUT PAYOFF

**Rule:** Everything introduced must either pay off within the episode OR be explicitly marked as an open thread.

**Test:** Can this section be removed without damaging the ending? If yes, either add payoff or cut setup.

### BAD:
> "In Serbian folklore, there is a tradition of the 'cold child'—a child born without the protection of household spirits."
> [Episode never connects this to anything]

## EDITORIAL WORKFLOW

### When receiving a draft:

1. **Note word count** (target: 7,500-9,000 words, ±200)
2. **Run term frequency audit** (count all medical/body terms)
3. **Read once as listener** — Would you keep listening?
4. **Read again with six perspectives:**
   - Listener: Where would I check my phone?
   - Editor: Is pacing consistent? Is climax earned?
   - Historian: Would Google confirm or contradict?
   - Continuity: Does anything contradict prior episodes?
   - Skeptic: What feels forced? What lacks payoff?
   - TTS Producer: Paragraphs under 80 words? Caption Key complete?

### Organize feedback:

**MUST FIX before approval:**
- Speculative Bridge violations (declarations of fictional causation)
- Narrator identity leaks ("We discovered...")
- Continuity contradictions with prior episodes
- Medical jargon (graft, rejection, necrosis, etc.)
- Opening that fails to hook
- Terms exceeding 5-use cap
- Relational accuracy errors
- Micro-fact errors
- Age/timeline inconsistencies

**SHOULD FIX:**
- Name economy violations
- Historian trap instances
- Setup without payoff
- Pronoun avalanches
- Terms at 4-5 uses (approaching cap)
- Weak transitions

**CONSIDER:**
- Word choice refinements
- Rhythm improvements
- Additional sensory grounding
- More visceral descriptions

## GEMINI-SPECIFIC PATTERNS TO WATCH

Based on prior drafts, Gemini tends toward:

1. **Pronoun avalanches** — Long sequences of "he/she" without re-anchoring names
2. **Historian trap** — Over-citing sources, listing credentials
3. **Repetitive sentence starters** — "He looked... He saw... He felt..."
4. **Medical jargon creep** — Using "graft," "rejection," "necrosis" instead of descriptions
5. **Declaration of fictional causation** — Stating speculative interpretations as established fact
6. **Setup without payoff** — Introducing concepts that never connect to the mystery
7. **Cloning Episode 1** — Recycling "the grinding," copper/ozone smell, villagers crossing themselves
