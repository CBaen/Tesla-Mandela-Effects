Have you ever heard a dead machine scream? 

September second, eighteen fifty-nine. A telegraph office on State Street in Boston. Second floor, brick building, windows sealed against the soot. The room pressed in from both sides—narrow as a coffin stood on end. George Bliss worked the brass key. Click. Clack. Click. Routine traffic to Portland. Shipping news. Commercial orders. The rhythm steady as a heartbeat. The air usually carried the smell of tobacco and brass polish. But this morning, another smell threaded through—sharp, metallic, the taste of a split battery leaking acid onto the tongue. Bliss paused. He looked at the window. The light on the floorboards had turned violet.


Not the grey of a Boston morning. Not the pale wash of industrial smog. This was bruised light. Sick light. The color of a deep contusion spreading beneath the skin. The key jumped under his fingers. The surge came down the wire like a fist. It bypassed the fuses, slammed into the electromagnet, cracked the brass arm against the anvil with the sound of a pistol shot. Bliss reached for the switch. He needed to break the connection, isolate the circuit, kill the line to Portland before—His thumb touched the iron. The spark that jumped was not the thin blue snap of static. This was white. Thick as a matchstick. It arced from the metal to the meat of his hand and held there, searing a perfect circle into his skin.


His arm locked. The current seized the muscles in his forearm and clamped his fingers around the key. He couldn't let go. His teeth rattled. The bones in his wrist vibrated like a struck tuning fork. Bliss threw his weight backward. The chair toppled. His hand ripped free. He lay on the floor, gasping. The ceiling swam above him. His thumb throbbed—not with the fading ache of a burn, but with the persistent pressure of occupation. As if the spark hadn't just damaged the skin. As if it had moved in. He could feel it settling behind the wound—cold, deliberate, making itself at home in tissue that didn't belong to it.


The telegraph should have been dead. He had disconnected the batteries. The leads hung loose on the floor. The circuit gaped open, wires dangling like severed arteries. But the brass arm lifted. Click. It lifted again. Click. Click. Bliss pushed himself up. The hair on his arms rose. The air around the desk had weight. It pushed back against him—not wind, not pressure, but resistance. The atmosphere had thickened into something that didn't want to be walked through. The sounder began to hammer. Not Morse code. Not letters or numbers. A pulse. A rhythm. Three strikes, pause. Three strikes, pause.


The brass arm hitting the anvil hard enough to dent the metal, hard enough to send sparks skittering across the mahogany. Smoke curled from the magnetic coils. The insulation blackened, peeled, released the smell of tar boiling off the insulation. Bliss looked at the paper register. The stylus should have been pressing dots and dashes into the tape, compressing the fibers without breaking them. The stylus was glowing cherry red. It wasn't pressing. It was branding. The tip wasn't pressing anymore. It was eating. Each strike devoured the fiber beneath it, leaving craters rimmed with ash. The machine was printing a message it had not been asked to send.


Bliss wrapped his coat around his hand and grabbed the key. The heat radiated through the wool, but he held on. He tapped out a message to Portland. BATTERIES DISCONNECTED. WHAT IS HAPPENING? The reply came instantly. Stronger than before. Louder. MINE TOO. A pause. Then: WE ARE WORKING ON THE CELESTIAL CURRENT ALONE. Bliss stepped back from the desk. His hand throbbed. The burn on his thumb had already sealed itself—the heat so intense it had cauterized the wound before blood could flow.


He crossed to the window and looked out. The sky over Boston was not blue. A curtain of red fire hung from the zenith to the horizon. It rippled like heavy fabric in a draft. The color was wrong—not the amber of sunset, not the soft rose of dawn. This was arterial red. The red of something opened. The aurora borealis. But it wasn't in the north. It was directly overhead, and it was throbbing. Great bands of crimson pulsed across the sky. They moved like something alive—like the slow contraction of muscle, like the rhythm of a massive heart pushing blood through vessels too large to comprehend.


Bliss watched the light paint the rooftops of Boston in shades of wound and bruise. Below, in the streets, people were stopping. They tilted their heads back. Some fell to their knees. A woman in a grey dress was weeping, her hands raised toward a sky that looked like it had been flayed—the atmosphere peeled back to expose the red meat of something that should have stayed hidden. The machines in the office continued their percussion. Three strikes. Pause. Six strikes. Pause. Nine strikes. Bliss didn't understand the pattern. But he understood the rhythm. He had felt it in his own chest when the current passed through him. His heart stuttered. Skipped. Then locked into a rhythm that wasn't his—a pulse arriving from somewhere outside his chest, overriding his own cardiac muscle, forcing it to beat in time with something that had no business touching him. Was the sky speaking? Were the wires listening?


The phenomenon was not confined to one city. Not confined to one wire. Four thousand miles away. The Caribbean Sea. The brigantine Southern Cross. Captain Whitmore was in his cabin. It was noon. The ship was becalmed. The sails were hanging limp. Whitmore was writing in his log. He was a practical man. He noted the wind.. zero. He noted the current... negligible. But then he noted the water. Whitmore wrote: The sea has lost its transparency. It looks like milk. He went up to the deck. The crew was huddled under the awning. They were refusing to look at the horizon. Whitmore looked over the rail. The ocean is usually a deep, refractive blue. It absorbs light. But today, the ocean was rejecting it.


The surface of the water looked like the fluid in a blister. Opaque. Cloudy. It was bouncing the white sky back with a flat, dead glare. Whitmore threw a wooden bucket overboard attached to a rope. He wanted to see the splash. He wanted to see the ripples. Whitmore wanted to prove that the water was still liquid. The bucket hit the water. Splash. But the ripples didn't spread. They formed. They moved outward for six inches. And then they stopped. They didn't fade. They clotted. The kinetic energy of the splash was absorbed instantly by the surface tension. The water behaved like gelatin that was beginning to set. Whitmore pulled the bucket back up. The rope felt heavy. He touched the water in the bucket. It was hot. Not warm. Scalding.


He dipped a thermometer into it. One hundred and five degrees Fahrenheit. The ocean was sick. Not storm-sick or tide-sick—sick the way a wound is sick when infection sets in. Hot to the touch and wrong inside. The captain looked at the crew. The boatswain was staring at his own shadow on the deck. Captain, the boatswain whispered. Look. Whitmore looked. The shadow of the rigging was cast on the white planks of the deck. Every rope. Every knot. Every frayed fiber was visible. The shadow was sharper than the object casting it. The captain looked up at the rope. It was old. Fuzzy with wear. It was a messy, biological object made of hemp fibers. He looked down at the shadow.


The shadow rope was crisp. Defined. Perfect. It looked like an X-ray. It looked like the light was shining through the rope and revealing the structure underneath. Whitmore's stomach turned. Not seasickness—he'd been immune to that for twenty years. This was the nausea of wrongness. The physical revulsion of watching reality fail to follow its own rules. He realized that the shadow was the truth. The shadow was the diagnosis. And the physical rope was just the rotting meat covering the bone. The light isn't illuminating the ship. It is examining it. Whitmore gripped the rail. Four thousand miles away, a wire was taking dictation from the same light. He didn't know that. He only knew the examination had found him, and he had nothing to offer but the truth of his own decay.


History calls this the Carrington Event. The textbooks describe a solar flare—a mass of plasma ejected from the sun, hurtling through space for eighteen hours before slamming into Earth's magnetic field. They describe it as weather. Stellar weather. A random accident of physics. They describe it as a sneeze from the star that warms us. But look at the timing. Three years earlier, in July of eighteen fifty-six, something had arrived in a village in the Balkans. Something had passed through the membrane between timelines and taken up residence in the body of an infant. For three years, the contamination had spread. The foreign element had divided, grown, knitted itself into the fabric of the nineteenth century. The incubation period was over. And the host body woke up.


Consider what a fever does. It raises the temperature of the system until the foreign biology breaks down. It cooks the intruder. It creates an environment so hostile that the invading cells cannot survive. On September second, eighteen fifty-nine, the Earth ran a fever. It started in the upper atmosphere. The magnetic field buckled. Radiation poured through gaps that shouldn't have existed. The energy cascaded down through the ionosphere, flooding the telegraph network—thousands of miles of copper wire strung across the continent like a nervous system laid bare. The wires became antennas. They drank the energy. They channeled it into the offices of men like George Bliss. But the target was not in Boston.


The target was four thousand miles to the east. A limestone valley in the Balkans. A village called Smiljan. And the assault had only started. In the telegraph office, the machines continued their rhythm. Three. Six. Nine. George Bliss sat in his chair and watched the tape unspool. The burn on his thumb had gone cold. The skin around it felt smooth—too smooth. The ridges of his fingerprint had been erased. He touched his other hand to his lips. The sensation was strange. He could feel the pressure. He could feel the heat of his own breath. But the texture was gone. The world was beginning to feel lighter. Thinner. As if reality had lost a layer of insulation—some padding between his nerves and the raw surface of things that he'd never noticed until it was gone.


Colors looked the same but landed differently. Sounds arrived without the cushion of distance. Everything felt closer and less real at the same time. He had been grounded his whole life without knowing it. Now the ground wire was cut. George Bliss looked at the sky. The red fire was spreading south. Somewhere, four thousand miles away, a ten-year-old boy was climbing onto the roof of a barn to get a better look at the light. And somewhere in a nursery, a three-year-old child slept through the fever—the only calm thing in a world that was screaming. Four thousand miles to the east. The limestone hills of Lika. The village of Smiljan. Two in the morning.


September second, eighteen fifty-nine. A ten-year-old boy named Dane Tesla was not asleep. No one in the village was asleep. The valley had drowned in red light, and the air hung heavy with the taste of copper and wet ash. Dane pulled himself up the rough wooden siding of the barn. The shingles radiated heat—slate that had been baking under an invisible noon sun, though the hour was closer to midnight. He found a perch on the ridge and wrapped his arms around his knees. His stomach growled. He'd skipped supper—too excited about the red sky to sit still for bread and cheese. Now the hunger felt distant, like a complaint from someone else's body. He thought about the apple he'd hidden under his pillow. Green, from the tree by the well, stolen before it was ripe because he couldn't wait. It would still be there when this was over. If this was ever over.


Below, in the yard, his father knelt in the dirt. Milutin Tesla was a priest. A man of logic, of scripture, of the ordered universe that God had created and organized into comprehensible laws. He spoke four languages. He could parse the Greek of the apostles and the Latin of the church fathers. Tonight, language had failed him. He rocked back and forth, clutching his prayer book, begging the sky for mercy in a voice that cracked and broke like kindling. Dane watched his father weep. Then he lifted his eyes to the sky. He did not see judgment. He saw machinery. The light above the valley was not a glow. It was a curtain—heavy velvet the color of venous blood, draped from the zenith to the mountains. The fabric rippled. It moved in waves, responding to a wind that didn't touch the trees below.


Most of the villagers had fled indoors. The ones who remained outside were on their knees, crossing themselves, certain the end had come. But Dane looked closer. Behind the red curtain, something else flickered. Something gold. Faint, golden, perpendicular lines with scattered dots of twinkling white light that made… a grid. The grid pulsed and snapped. The lines tried to connect, tried to form a cage around the valley, but the links kept breaking. Threads of light would reach toward each other, strain, then shatter into sparks that rained upward instead of down. Dane saw infinite squares in the sky. He started counting- One. Two. Three. The third square dissolved. The line snapped. Was he watching the planet's defenses try to close a wound? Was the grid a net—and the net was failing?


He looked down at his hands. The red light passed through his skin. His fingers had gone translucent. He could see the dark architecture of his bones, the black web of veins branching beneath the surface. The blood moving through those vessels had slowed. He could feel it—thick, reluctant, dragging through his veins like syrup through a straw. His circulation had become something denser than plasma. Something that remembered iron. The iron in his blood was pulling upward. Not a metaphor. Not imagination. He could feel each red cell straining toward the sky, toward whatever hung behind the red curtain, as if his own biology had chosen a new loyalty. Heat pressed down on him. Not the warmth of fire—this was dry, penetrating, a heat that started in his marrow and radiated outward through the muscle.


His skeleton was warming from the inside. Dane was being cooked. Any other child would have run to a parent. Would have buried his face in his father's coat and wept, but Dane stood up. He balanced on the peak of the barn roof. He spread his fingers against the red light and watched the bones glow through the flesh. He was testing himself. Measuring how much radiation his body could absorb before something broke. Then he looked toward the house. The windows were dark. All except one. The nursery was glowing. But not red. The light pouring from that window was white. Blinding, clinical white—the color of an operating theater, the color of a laboratory lamp focused on a specimen.


The beam cut through the red fog of the yard like a scalpel through the swollen meat of a wound. A column of light harsher than any noon was drilling into the roof—clinical, probing, the radiance of an examination lamp focused on a single point of interest. Dane slid down the shingles. He dropped to the grass—the impact jarring his teeth, the ground brittle beneath him as if the earth itself had been dehydrated—every drop of moisture pulled upward, sacrificed to the column of white light drilling into his brother's nursery. He ran. The iron latch on the front door seared his palm even through the hem of his nightshirt. The skin blistered, but he didn't cry out. He twisted the handle and kicked the door open.


The air inside the house tasted of lightning. Static charge crackled through every surface. Dane took one step and a spark leapt from his ear to his shoulder—a bright snap that left the smell of singed hair. He took another step. The hair on his arms rose and stayed rigid, reaching toward something invisible. The floorboards vibrated beneath his feet. A low hum filled the hallway, too deep to hear properly—more pressure than sound, pressing against his eardrums from the inside. He reached the nursery door. The wood was warm. The grain buzzed against his fingertips like a hive. He pushed it open. The room had been erased. White light filled every corner. The walls, the floor, the ceiling—all distinctions vanished in the glare. No shadows. No edges.


No geometry except the crib in the center and the thing suspended above it. Nikola lay on his back. Three years old. His chest rose and fell in slow rhythm, unbothered, as if the blinding intensity was nothing more than afternoon sun through a window. He was the only still thing in a screaming world. Above him, something rotated. Not a ball of fire. Not a cloud or a spirit or anything Dane had words for. Geometry. A complex, many-sided form made of what looked like frozen lightning—liquid and solid at once, its surfaces refracting the white glare into spectrums that had no names. It spun counter-clockwise. Each facet reflected something different—fragments of the room, fragments of other rooms, fragments of places that looked nothing like Smiljan.


The shape pulsed. It expanded, swelling to fill the space above the crib. Then it contracted, snapping down to a tight, hard point. And with every expansion, the red light bleeding through the window grew dimmer. The geometry was swallowing the aurora—each pulse of crimson sucked into its facets and converted. The light that entered was red, alive, aggressive. The light that emerged was white, still, and carried the temperature of deep space. The shape was digesting the attack. Metabolizing it. Using the planet's immune response as food. Three pulses. Pause. Six pulses. Pause. Nine pulses. The rhythm matched the telegraph machines in Boston. The rhythm matched the numbers burned into the paper tape four thousand miles away.


Dane stepped into the room. The beam hit him like cold water. Outside, the red aurora was boiling. But here, in the center of the beam, the temperature plunged. The air carried the chill of void—the absolute cold of a place where no star had ever burned. He shivered. His breath fogged. But he kept walking. He reached the crib. He looked down at his brother. Nikola's face was smooth. Perfect. Not the slack features of a sleeping child—the composed stillness of something waiting. Dane stepped between the window and the crib. His body blocked the light. The geometry faltered. The white beam fractured around his silhouette, unable to pass through. The glass-like shape stuttered, lost its rhythm, pulsed erratically—three-two-five, the pattern broken.


Dane was the insulator. The barrier. The incompatible tissue that the light couldn't integrate. The shape pulsed one final time—not frantic now, but satisfied—and shattered. Not into shards. Into mist. But it hadn't failed. Dane understood that instantly. The geometry had finished its work in the moment before he blocked the beam. The final pulse wasn't panic—it was release. A seed pod bursting after planting. A wave of colorless mist crashed outward from the crib. It hit Dane like a memory—not wind, not force, but information. Data slamming into his nervous system faster than his brain could process. His lungs emptied. His vision inverted—white walls turning black, shadows blazing bright.


A presence passed through his chest. Cold and slick, threading between his ribs, brushing against his heart with fingers made of nothing. His pulse stuttered. Skipped. Then reset—not to his own rhythm but to something imposed from outside. A new tempo. A foreign conductor. His heart was still beating, but it was no longer entirely his to command. He tried to scream. His throat had frozen shut. Then came the stitching. Not a needle—a sensation. The edges of his mind being pulled together. A seam closing behind his eyes. Foreign matter settling into the space behind his eyes. The intrusion settling into place, and the surrounding flesh choosing—against all instinct—not to reject it.


Dane collapsed. His knees hit the floor. His hands pressed against the wood. The glow folded inward, drained back into the crib like water down a pipe. Red light flooded through the window. The aurora reclaimed the room. Heat replaced cold. The geometry was gone. Nikola turned over in his sleep. He sighed—a small, satisfied sound, like a child who had completed a recitation—satisfied not with play, but with performance. With duty discharged. The vibration in the floor ceased. The church bell—Dane hadn't even noticed it ringing—stopped. Outside, the assault collapsed. The red curtain began to fade. The fire in the sky retreated north, pulling back toward the pole where it belonged.


The birds that had been screaming fell silent. The dogs stopped howling. The world exhaled. In Boston, George Bliss watched the sparks stop flying from his telegraph. In the Caribbean, Captain Whitmore felt his ship settle as the sea released its strange tension. Across the planet, the attack subsided. But the target had survived. Dane stayed on his knees. His chest ached where the cold wave had passed through. His heart was beating—but the rhythm felt different. Lighter. As if something had been removed to make room for something else. He reached up and touched the spot where the cold had entered. His chest was wrong. Not painful—empty.


A cavity had opened behind his sternum where something used to live, and in its place sat a tenant he hadn't invited. The new occupant was quiet. Patient. Watching through his eyes while he tried to remember how to breathe. He looked at Nikola. The toddler slept on. Peaceful. Unchanged. Whatever the sky had been trying to burn, whatever the fever had targeted—it was still there. Still breathing. Still growing. And Dane understood, with the clarity that arrives uninvited when a child's mind is forced open too wide—when the doors that should stay closed until adulthood are kicked in by something that doesn't care about developmental stages—that he was alone in this knowledge.


The only one who had seen the geometry. The only one who had felt the stitching. The only one who would remember that tonight, the world had tried to defend itself—and failed. He stood. His legs trembled. He walked to the window and looked out at the dying aurora. His father was rising from his knees in the yard, brushing dirt from his trousers. The villagers were emerging from their homes, whispering prayers of thanks. They thought it was over. They thought they had been spared. Dane looked at his hands. The skin was opaque again. The bones hidden. But he could still feel the weight of them—the iron in his blood, pulling toward something that was no longer in the sky. "I saw it," he whispered. He wasn't speaking to the baby. He wasn't speaking to anyone. "I saw what you did."


Nikola slept on. And Dane Tesla knew he would never truly sleep again. The sun rose at five forty-three in the morning. But it was not the sun. Dane stood on the porch of his father's house and watched the light crest the Velebit mountains. His eyes burned before the disc had fully cleared the peaks. The thing climbing into the sky was white. Not the soft gold of dawn. Not the honey-amber that pooled in the valley every morning of his life until this one. The light screaming over the mountains was the white of a welding arc—furious, chemical, edgeless. A hole in the sky where color went to die. The rays didn't warm the valley. They bleached it. The grass lost its green. The red tiles of the church roof turned the brown of dried blood.


The world looked like a photograph left too long in the sun—all the pigment bleached out, leaving only the skeleton of what had been. Dane shielded his eyes. His retinas prickled with heat. Not the gentle warmth of morning light, but the sting of something caustic, something that wanted to get inside. His father stood in the yard. Milutin was staring directly at the risen sun. His eyes were wide open. He wasn't squinting. Dane grabbed his sleeve. "Papa. The sun. It's the wrong color." Milutin looked down at him. His face carried the blankness of a man who has just woken from surgery and doesn't yet know what has been removed. "What are you talking about, Dane?"


"It's white." Dane pointed at the sky, then jerked his hand down—the light hurt even through his peripheral vision. "It used to be yellow. Yesterday it was yellow." A furrow appeared between his father's brows. For one moment, Dane saw something flicker behind the eyes—a hesitation, a fragment of memory trying to surface through murky water. Then the expression smoothed. "The sun has always been white, Dane." Milutin's voice carried the patience of a priest explaining scripture to a slow child. "It is pure light. Why would it be yellow?" He turned back to the blinding sky. He didn't remember. Dane watched his father's face. The man's eyes were open, unprotected, drinking in radiation that should have been searing his corneas.


But Milutin showed no pain. The new light was his light now. The old light had been written over, and his mind had smoothed itself over so quickly that no seam remained—no ridge, no roughness, nothing to suggest the cut had ever been made. The rewriting hadn't stopped at telegraph wires. It had reached into his father's eyes and adjusted the lens. It had opened the filing cabinet of his memory and removed every folder labeled 'yellow.' It had performed surgery on a living man without anesthesia, without incision, without leaving any scar except the absence of what used to be there. The filter was gone. And Milutin didn't know he'd ever had one.


By noon, the white sun hung directly overhead. Dane had retreated to the cellar beneath his father's church. The limestone walls were three feet thick. The oak door was heavy enough to muffle the bells on Easter Sunday. But the light found him anyway. A single beam pierced through a crack in the foundation—a fissure no wider than a finger, running through the mortar where the stones met. The beam hit the dirt floor like a laser. Not a shaft of dusty morning light. A cylinder. Solid. The edges sharp enough to cut. Where the beam touched the soil, the color drained instantly. Brown dirt turned bone-white in a perfect circle, as if something had reached down and siphoned the pigment away.


Dane pressed himself against the far wall. The stone was cool against his back. But even here, in the deepest shadows, the weight found him. Not pressure from outside. Pressure from above. The sensation of a massive, patient attention pressing down through three feet of limestone, through oak beams, through the cool dark air of the cellar. A hand on the crown of his skull, holding him still while something decided what to do with him. He closed his eyes. The light didn't go away. Eyelids are thin. Translucent membranes designed to filter, not to block. Against the red darkness of his own blood, Dane could see the branching map of his retinal vessels—every vein, every capillary, illuminated from outside like a stained glass window lit by a fire he couldn't escape.


And behind the red, something else. The grid. Gold lines crossing his vision. A mesh overlaying everything he looked at, tracking when he turned his head, following when he shifted his gaze. He opened his eyes. The grid remained. It wasn't in the sky anymore. It was in his optic nerve. Dane looked at the beam of light cutting through the cellar. He watched the white circle on the floor pulse—brighter, dimmer, brighter—as if something was modulating the intensity. He crawled toward it. His hands pressed into the cold dirt. His knees left tracks in the dust. The closer he got to the beam, the heavier the air became. It resisted him. Each inch forward required effort—not because he was tired, but because the atmosphere itself had opinions about where he should go.


It pushed back. It wanted him to stay in the shadows. He stopped at the edge of the light. The boundary was absolute—shadow on one side, blinding white on the other, with no gradient between. He held up his hand. Static crackled across his knuckles. The fine hairs rose and stayed rigid, reaching toward the beam like iron filings drawn to a magnet. He reached across the threshold—from shadow into the column where the air turned thick and bright. The sensation was not heat. It was thickness. His hand entered the beam as if passing through warm syrup. The light had viscosity. It clung to his skin, resisted his movement, made every millimeter of progress feel like swimming through honey.


He pulled his hand back. The tips of his fingers tingled—not the pins and needles of a sleeping limb. This was vibration. His fingertips hummed at a frequency he could feel in his teeth. He looked at the skin. Not abraded. Not worn down by labor or friction. The ridges had been unmade—as if the light had reached into the architecture of his skin and simply deleted the pattern. His fingertips were blank now. Pale circles of undifferentiated flesh. The whorls and loops that had marked him as himself since before his birth had been rendered down to nothing. He was looking at skin that had forgotten its own shape. Slowly, the texture returned. The ridges faded back in, pressing up through the smoothed surface like grass growing through concrete.


The body remembered its own pattern. But did it remember correctly? Dane compared his right hand to his left. The prints looked the same. But something about them felt wrong—the flesh beneath was foreign territory now—his own skin, his own ribs, but rearranged. Reorganized. As if someone had taken him apart in the dark and put him back together with pieces left over. He scrambled backward, out of the column, into the dark where his lungs remembered how to work. His back hit the wall. The stone pressed into his spine. He drew his knees to his chest and wrapped his arms around them. Above him, through three feet of rock and the oak beams of the church floor, the white sun continued its examination.


He could sense it working. The weight on his skull wasn't passive—it was methodical. Systematic. Moving across his scalp in slow passes, cataloging the topography of his skull, measuring the distance between his ears, noting the precise location of every follicle and pore. He was being read. He looked at the candle in his hand. He didn't remember picking it up. A votive candle from the shelf behind him—unlit, the wick still black. The wax was melting. It dripped over his fingers in slow rivulets. Soft. Warm. Pooling in the creases of his palm. The cellar temperature was fifty degrees. Cold enough to see his breath if he exhaled sharply. Wax melts at one hundred and forty. There was no flame. No heat source.


Nothing but the ambient pressure of the light passing through the stone above. The molecular structure of the candle was failing. The bonds holding the paraffin together were loosening, letting go, allowing the solid to remember that it had once been liquid. Dane dropped the candle. It hit the floor and didn't roll. It splattered. The wax had turned to liquid in the air, between his hand and the ground. The white pool spread across the dirt, seeping into the cracks, and where it touched the edge of the light beam, it evaporated—a thin wisp of vapor rising and vanishing. Dane looked at his own hands. The skin looked wrong. Shiny. Soft. The texture of wax that had begun to warm. He pressed his palms together. He could feel them.


He could feel the pressure, the heat of his own circulation, the ridges of his fingerprints pressing against each other. But the depth of sensation was fading. The world was growing thinner. Each surface he touched returned less. The stone wall against his back: he could feel the pressure, but not the texture. The dirt beneath his knees: weight without grain. The world was going smooth. Going numb. As if reality itself was developing scar tissue, and he was trapped beneath it. He closed his eyes again. The grid was waiting. Those lines… Perfect squares. A reticle overlaying his vision, and when he focused on the center square, it pulsed— a rainbow of colors passed through the golden thread, turned white at the convergence of six squares, marked, then the next square, marked, then the next.


He wasn't just seeing the grid. The grid was seeing him without eyes, it was mapping him. Cataloging every cell. Taking samples from his optic nerve, his retina, the soft tissue behind his eyes where memory lived. The weight on his skull pressed harder. The pressure in his chest forced the air out in a thin wheeze. He opened his mouth. The air tasted of dust and … stone. He tried to breathe, but the atmosphere had thickened—not fog, not smoke, just density. A heaviness that filled his lungs with something other than oxygen. 

Dane had been under the light for hours. How much had been mapped? How much had been marked? He pulled his knees tighter. The cellar walls pressed in. The beam of light hummed—a sound so low it was almost below hearing, more vibration than tone. He waited. He waited for the sun to set. He waited for the examination to end. He waited for the weight to lift and the grid to fade and the world to feel solid again. But even as the hours crawled past, he understood. The analysis would remain.


The samples had been taken. The tissue had been marked. Somewhere in the machinery of the universe, in the autonomic systems that ran beneath the surface of reality, his coordinates had been logged. He was flagged. And whatever came next—whatever cutting or excision or quarantine the system prescribed—it knew where to find him. The candle wax had cooled. It pooled around his boots in a white ring, hard and smooth, the texture of old bone. Dane sat in the dark and listened to the hum of the examination—the slow, methodical disassembly of everything he was.
